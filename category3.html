The Rise of
Sovereign Wealth Funds.

How contemporary design is shifting away from communal maximalism to embrace the necessity of private, silent spaces.

By Karel Macek January 12, 2026

"In the rush of digital noise, the most luxurious thing we can build is a room where nothing happens."

For the better part of the last decade, architectural trends favored the "open plan"—a sprawling, boundary-less philosophy designed to encourage spontaneous collaboration. However, as our digital lives have become increasingly permeable, our physical spaces are beginning to push back.

New projects in Stockholm and Tokyo are seeing a return to the "cell"—small, highly specialized rooms designed for a single purpose: focus. These are not the cold, utilitarian cubicles of the 1980s, but rather warm, tactile sanctuaries.

The Materiality of Silence

Texture plays a critical role in this shift. Architects are opting for sound-dampening felt walls, raw cedar, and unpolished stone. The goal is to create a sensory "dead zone" that allows the inhabitant to disconnect from the frantic pace of the outside world.
